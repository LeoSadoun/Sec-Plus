## Official Definition
Non-mandatory recommendations and best practices that provide flexibility and guidance for achieving security goals.

## Key Concepts/Technical Details
*   **Discretionary:** Unlike policies or standards, guidelines are not strictly enforced but are highly recommended.
*   **Advice:** Provides suggestions on how to meet a policy objective when a specific standard may not apply.
*   **Flexibility:** Allows for situational judgment while still steering behavior toward security.

## Real-World Examples
*   **Password Best Practices:** Suggesting the use of passphrases instead of complex strings (if the standard only mandates length).
*   **Physical Security:** Recommending that employees lock their screens when leaving their desks (if the "Clean Desk Policy" is general).
*   **Coding Guidelines:** Suggestions for developers on how to avoid common vulnerabilities like SQL injection.

## Exam Tips
*   **Keywords:** "Recommended," "Suggestions," "Advice," "Non-mandatory," "Flexible."
*   **Scenario:** If an organization suggests (but doesn't require) that employees use a specific brand of privacy screen, it is a **Guideline**.
*   **Distinction:** If you *must* do it, it's a Policy/Standard; if you *should* do it, it's a Guideline.

## Relationship to other concepts
*   **Security Awareness:** Guidelines are often used in training to encourage better security habits.
*   **Culture:** Guidelines help shape a security-conscious culture without being overly restrictive.
