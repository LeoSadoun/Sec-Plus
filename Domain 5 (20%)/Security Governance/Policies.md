## Official Definition
A high-level document that outlines an organization's requirements and expectations for security, serving as the foundation for the security program.

## Key Concepts/Technical Details
*   **Mandatory:** Policies are non-negotiable and must be followed by all employees and contractors.
*   **AUP (Acceptable Use Policy):** Defines the "do's and don'ts" for using organizational assets (e.g., email, internet, hardware).
*   **Information Security Policy (ISP):** The master document governing the entire security program.
*   **Privacy Policy:** Describes how the organization handles PII (Personally Identifiable Information).
*   **Separation of Duties:** Ensures no single individual has total control over a critical process.
*   **Least Privilege:** Users are granted only the access necessary to perform their jobs.

## Real-World Examples
*   **Password Policy:** Requiring MFA and minimum character lengths.
*   **Clean Desk Policy:** Ensuring sensitive data is not left exposed on physical desks.
*   **Remote Work Policy:** Dictating the use of VPNs and company-issued devices for telecommuting.

## Exam Tips
*   **Keywords:** "Mandatory," "High-level," "Strategic," "Foundation."
*   **Scenario:** If a user is caught using work email for personal business, they have likely violated the **AUP**.
*   **Sign-off:** New hires usually must sign the AUP before receiving account access.

## Relationship to other concepts
*   **Risk Management:** Policies are the first step in mitigating risk by setting organizational boundaries.
*   **Compliance:** Policies are often written to ensure the organization meets legal requirements (e.g., HIPAA, GDPR).
