## Official Definition
**Internal Audit:** An audit performed by the organization’s own staff (or contractors hired by them) to monitor internal controls and prepare for external reviews.
**External Audit:** An audit performed by an independent third party (e.g., a regulatory body or a specialized firm) to provide objective assurance to stakeholders.

## Key Concepts/Technical Details
*   **Objectivity:** External audits are more objective because the auditors have no personal stake in the company.
*   **Scope:** Defined by the audit charter or engagement letter.
*   **Evidence:** Includes log files, policy documents, screenshots, and physical inspections.
*   **Finding:** A specific instance where a control was found to be ineffective or missing.
*   **Remediation:** The steps taken by the organization to fix the findings before the next audit.

## Real-World Examples
*   **Internal Audit:** The internal IT team performing a monthly check of user access rights to ensure terminated employees have been removed.
*   **External Audit:** The IRS auditing a company's financial security controls, or a firm like EY performing a SOC 2 audit.
*   **Regulatory Audit:** A surprise inspection by a government agency (e.g., HIPAA audit).

## Exam Tips
*   **Keywords:** "Independent (External)," "Self-check (Internal)," "Objectivity," "Finding," "Remediation."
*   **Scenario:** To prepare for an upcoming regulatory review, the CISO orders an **Internal Audit**.
*   **Conflict of Interest:** Internal auditors should report to the Board or CEO, not the CIO, to maintain independence.

## Relationship to other concepts
*   **Compliance:** Audits are the primary mechanism for verifying compliance with laws and regulations.
*   **Risk Management:** Audit findings are added to the Risk Register to ensure they are tracked and remediated.
