## Official Definition
A decentralized, distributed, and public digital ledger that is used to record transactions across many computers so that the record cannot be altered retroactively.

## Key Concepts/Technical Details
*   **Decentralization:** No single central authority (like a bank) controls the data; it is distributed across a network of nodes.
*   **Immutability:** Once a block of data is added to the chain, it cannot be changed without changing all subsequent blocks (which is computationally impossible).
*   **Hashing:** Each block contains a hash of the previous block, creating a secure link (the "chain").
*   **Consensus Mechanisms:**
    *   **Proof of Work (PoW):** Nodes (miners) solve complex mathematical puzzles to validate transactions.
    *   **Proof of Stake (PoS):** Validators are chosen based on the number of coins they hold and are willing to "stake."
*   **Public vs. Private Ledger:** Public (permissionless) like Bitcoin; Private (permissioned) used within an organization.

## Real-World Examples
*   **Cryptocurrency:** Bitcoin and Ethereum.
*   **Supply Chain:** Tracking the origin and movement of goods (e.g., food safety tracking).
*   **Smart Contracts:** Self-executing contracts with the terms directly written into code.
*   **Digital Identity:** Securely managing and verifying identities without a central provider.

## Exam Tips
*   **Immutable Ledger:** This is the defining characteristic of blockchain.
*   **Decentralized:** No single point of failure.
*   **Integrity:** Blockchain is primarily about ensuring the integrity of the transaction history.
*   **Double-Spending:** Blockchain solves this problem in digital currencies without a central bank.

## Relationship to other concepts
*   **Integrity:** One of the strongest implementations of data integrity.
*   **Availability:** High availability due to the distributed nature of the nodes.
*   **Hashing:** The core cryptographic technology that powers blockchain.
