### Official Definition
Controls that focus on the management of risk and the management of information system security through administrative actions, policies, and procedures.

### Key Characteristics
- **Governance-Focused:** Aligns security with business objectives.
- **Paper-Based:** Often involves documentation, planning, and oversight.
- **High-Level:** Set by leadership to guide the entire organization.
- **Compliance:** Frequently used to meet legal or regulatory requirements.

### Real-World Examples
1. **Security Policies:** High-level documents defining the organization's security posture.
2. **Risk Assessments:** The process of identifying, analyzing, and evaluating risks.
3. **Personnel Security:** Background checks, onboarding, and offboarding procedures.
4. **Security Awareness Training:** Formal programs to educate employees on security risks.
5. **Vendor Risk Management:** Establishing security requirements for third-party partners.

### Exam Tips
- **Keywords:** "Policy," "Procedure," "Assessment," "Planning," "Governance," "Compliance," "Administrative."
- **Pitfall:** Managerial controls are the "brain" of the operation; they don't *do* the security, they *direct* it.
- **Interaction:** A Managerial control (Policy) dictates the implementation of a Technical control (Encryption).

### Relationship to other controls
- **Directive:** Policies (Managerial) direct employees on how to handle data.
- **Preventive:** Background checks (Managerial) prevent the hiring of high-risk individuals.
- **Operational:** Change management policies (Managerial) define how operational changes should occur.
