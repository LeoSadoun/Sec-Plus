## Official Definition
A schema used to categorize data based on its sensitivity and the impact on the organization if it were disclosed, altered, or destroyed.

## Key Characteristics/Technical Details
*   **Classification Levels (Commercial):**
    *   **Public:** Available to everyone.
    *   **Sensitive:** Internal data that requires some protection.
    *   **Private:** Personal information (PII/PHI).
    *   **Confidential:** Most sensitive; trade secrets, intellectual property.
*   **Classification Levels (Government/Military):**
    *   **Unclassified:** Available to the public.
    *   **Confidential:** Disclosure would cause damage to national security.
    *   **Secret:** Disclosure would cause *serious* damage.
    *   **Top Secret:** Disclosure would cause *exceptionally grave* damage.
*   **Labeling:** The physical or digital tag applied to data indicating its classification (Metadata).
*   **Clearance:** The level of access granted to an individual based on a background check and need-to-know.

## Real-World Examples
*   **Public:** The company's annual report published on its website.
*   **Confidential (Commercial):** The secret recipe for a soft drink or a future product roadmap.
*   **Top Secret:** Information about nuclear weapon designs or high-level intelligence sources.

## Exam Tips
*   **Keywords:** Labeling, Metadata, Sensitivity, Impact, Clearance, Need-to-know.
*   **Scenario:** If disclosure would cause "grave damage" to the nation, what is the classification? Top Secret.
*   **Key Rule:** Clearance alone doesn't grant access; the individual must also have a "Need-to-Know."

## Relationship to other concepts
*   **Access Control:** Classification and clearance are the basis for Mandatory Access Control (MAC).
*   **DLP:** Data Loss Prevention systems use classification labels to decide whether to block a file from being emailed or copied.
