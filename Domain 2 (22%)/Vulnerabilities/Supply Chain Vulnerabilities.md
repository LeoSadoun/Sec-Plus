## Official Definition
* **Supply Chain Vulnerability:** A weakness in the network of entities (vendors, developers, manufacturers) involved in creating and delivering a product or service.

## Key Characteristics/Technical Details
* **Third-Party Dependencies:** Vulnerabilities in open-source libraries or commercial components used within a software product.
* **Lack of Vendor Oversight:** Not auditing the security practices of critical suppliers.
* **Compromised Development Environments:** Attackers breaching a vendor's build pipeline to inject malware into legitimate software.
* **Hardware Interdiction:** Vulnerabilities introduced during the manufacturing or shipping of physical components.
* **End-of-Life (EOL) Components:** Using components that are no longer supported by their original manufacturer.

## Real-World Examples
* **Kaseya VSA Attack (2021):** Attackers exploited a vulnerability in Kaseya's remote monitoring tool to deploy ransomware to hundreds of its MSP customers' clients.
* **Log4j (Log4Shell):** A vulnerability in a ubiquitous open-source library that created a massive, global supply chain crisis.

## Exam Tips
* **Keywords:** Third-party, Open-source, SBOM, Vendor risk, Upstream, Build pipeline.
* **Scenario:** A "vulnerability found in a common library" or a "compromised software update from a trusted vendor."

## Relationship to other concepts
* **Threat Vectors:** Exploited via Supply Chain Vectors.
* **Mitigation:** Software Bill of Materials (SBOM), vendor audits, and supply chain security frameworks.
